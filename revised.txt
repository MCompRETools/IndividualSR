1 Key Concepts
1.1 Individual Dimension: Definitions
As defined in the Karlskrona manifesto, individual sustainability refers to the
maintenance of the private goods of individual human capital. The human
capital consists of health, education, skills, knowledge, leadership, and access
to services. Individual dimension covers individual freedom, agency, human
dignity, and fulfilment.
Individual sustainability refers to longer-term changes in individuals’ atti-
tudes, aptitudes and/or behavior that result from their involvement with a par-
ticular sports development program or activity. This is achieved by evaluating
the consumption output using a consumer’s personal and economic well-being
as standards. Individual sustainability relies on a capacity for reflection and
an awareness of one’s past, present, and future to achieve positive living and
psychological well-being. It is a person’s ability to live a healthy lifestyle that
includes creating harmony, interconnections, community, ethically responsible
systems, and relatively high levels of awareness in one’s values, thoughts, and
behaviors.
The individual dimension covers individual freedom and agency (the ability
to act in an environment), human dignity and fulfillment. It includes the ability
of individuals to thrive, exercise their rights and develop freely.
Individual sustainability includes possessing a well-developed and demon-
strated value system that acknowledges the importance and interconnectedness
of all global biological and social systems, and our appropriate place within
them.
1.2 Human Abilities or Values Pertaining to Individual
Sustainability
Individual Sustainability Requirements (ISR) are system-specific requirements
derived from underlying human values and human abilities. Human values rep-
resent abstract principles that capture what individuals consider important, and
1
they play a central role in shaping decision-making and behavior. Values func-
tion as guiding criteria through which individuals justify actions, make choices,
and evaluate themselves, others, and events. These values may include aspects
such as achievement, prestige, and wealth, which, although not strictly ethical
in nature, significantly influence behavior and decisions. For example, when
Google employees urged their CEO to withdraw from Project Maven, their
arguments extended beyond moral responsibility to include concerns about po-
tential damage to the company’s reputation and its ability to attract talent.
This illustrates that values such as market reputation and public image—while
not traditionally categorized as ethical—are nonetheless influential and actively
guide decision-making.
Although values are organized in a universal and structured manner, indi-
viduals and groups differ substantially in the relative importance they assign
to different values. Building on this, Maio et al. conceptualize values as men-
tal representations operating across three interconnected levels: (1) L1 (Value
system level), which captures patterns and relationships among values held by
individuals; (2) L2 (Abstract interpretation level), where individuals interpret
what a value means in a given context (e.g., what constitutes “high-quality soft-
ware” as an expression of achievement); and (3) L3 (Behavioral level), where
values are instantiated through concrete actions and system behaviors. For in-
stance, a search engine company that prioritizes privacy may operationalize this
value by ensuring that user queries are never logged.
In this context, operationalizing human values in software refers to the pro-
cess of identifying relevant values and translating them into concrete, imple-
mentable, and measurable constructs that can be validated and verified within
a system. ISR serves as this operational bridge by converting abstract values
into system-level requirements.
An important empirical finding is that value hierarchies are context-dependent
and dynamic. Even for the same individual, the relative importance of values
may shift across domains. While some values, such as security or achievement,
tend to remain stable, others may change in significance or become irrelevant de-
pending on the application context. This highlights the need for domain-specific
elicitation and operationalization of values when deriving ISR.
1.2.1 Unified Characteristics of a Self-Actualized / Fully Function-
ing Individual
The following characteristics synthesize and de-duplicate the attributes pro-
posed by Maslow and Rogers, providing a consolidated view of a self-actualized
or fully functioning individual.
1.Realistic and Accurate Perception
Ability to perceive reality objectively, without distortion from biases, fears,
or social conditioning.
2.Acceptance of Self, Others, and Nature
Comfort with oneself and others without excessive guilt or judgment, along
2
with acceptance of natural processes.
3.Openness to Experience
Willingness to engage with new ideas, emotions, and experiences without
defensiveness.
4.Authenticity and Inner Congruence
Alignment between internal feelings and external behavior; acting in ways
true to oneself.
5.Spontaneity and Naturalness
Ability to act freely and genuinely without rigid adherence to social ex-
pectations.
6.Problem-Centered Orientation
Focus on meaningful goals beyond self-interest, with a commitment to
solving real-world or societal problems.
7.Autonomy and Inner Authority
Independence from external pressures, with decisions guided by internal
values rather than societal norms.
8.Resistance to Social Conditioning
Critical stance toward rigid norms and institutions when they conflict with
personal values.
9.Desire for Privacy and Reflection
Capacity for solitude, self-reflection, and independence from constant so-
cial interaction.
10.Deep Interpersonal Relationships and Intimacy
Ability to form meaningful, empathetic, and authentic relationships with
others.
11.Empathy and Identification with Humanity
Strong sense of connection, compassion, and responsibility toward others
and society.
12.Democratic and Egalitarian Attitude
Respect for all individuals and valuing equality and fairness in interactions.
13.Creativity and Expressiveness
Ability to think innovatively and express oneself in original and adaptive
ways.
14.Freshness of Appreciation
Capacity to experience everyday events with renewed wonder and emo-
tional richness.
3
15.Peak Experiences
Experiencing intense moments of joy, insight, or transcendence that pro-
vide meaning and fulfillment.
16.Holistic and Process-Oriented Self
Viewing life as a continuous evolving process and embracing growth and
change.
17.Value System Transformation
Shift toward intrinsic values such as growth, meaning, ethics, and self-
development.
18.Reduced Materialism
Lower emphasis on material possessions, prioritizing experiences and re-
lationships.
19.Spiritual or Existential Awareness
Interest in deeper meaning, purpose, or connection beyond the self.
20.Balanced View of Science and Technology
Critical yet reflective stance toward technology, recognizing both benefits
and limitations.
21.Ecological and Nature-Oriented Awareness
Respect and sensitivity toward nature and environmental interconnected-
ness.
Note: These characteristics are synthesized by merging overlapping concepts
from Maslow’s self-actualization theory and Rogers’ fully functioning person,
ensuring removal of redundancy while preserving conceptual completeness.

1.3 SuSAF Framework for Individual Sustainability
TheSusAFapproach proposes the following categories of sustainability dimensions-
Health
Health means the state of a person ’s mental or physical condition.
•How can the product or service improve or worsen a person’s physical,
mental, and/or emotional health?
6
•For example, can it make a person feel anything good or bad – e.g., (un-
der)valued, (dis)respected, (in)dependent, or coerced?
Lifelong Learning
Lifelong learning means the use of learning opportunities throughout people’s
lives for continuous development.
•How can the product or service affect people’s competencies?
Privacy
Privacy means being free from intrusion or disturbance in one’s private life.
•How can the product or service expose (or help to hide) a person’s identity,
whereabouts, or relations?
Safety
Safety means being protected from danger, risk, or injury.
•How can the product or service expose (or protect) a person from physical
harm?
•How can it make a person feel more (or less) exposed to harm?
•What if the product or service is used in an unintended way?
Self-awareness and Free Will
Self-awareness and free will mean the capacity of an individual to act or make
decisions on their own.
•How can the product or service empower (or prevent) a person from taking
an action or decision when necessary?
•Can those affected by the product or service understand its implications,
express concerns, or be represented by someone?
1.4 Human Values and Artificial Intelligence (AI)
As AI-driven systems grow more sophisticated and autonomous, they raise fun-
damental questions about alignment: not just whether the underlying AI mod-
els behave safely, but whether the software systems that embed and orchestrate
them uphold human values like trust, honesty, autonomy, fairness, and account-
ability.
One of the central challenges in addressing these values lies in the highly
subjective prioritization of values. This is especially significant for AI systems
due to their autonomy, opacity, and wide-reaching societal impact. Different
7
stakeholders (e.g., developers, users, regulators, domain experts) and impacted
communities often hold divergent or conflicting value priorities. For example,
a development team may prioritize system efficiency and scalability, while end
users may emphasize transparency in decision-making, equity, or preservation
of autonomy. Values such as sustainability, privacy, accountability, and inclu-
siveness are often interpreted and weighted differently, leading to miscommuni-
cation, overlooked needs, or unintended harms. Conflicts may arise within user
groups as well. For example, law enforcement may emphasize national secu-
rity, while civil rights groups prioritize privacy protections. Similarly, designers
might face a trade-off between creating highly secure systems and maintaining
ease of use for end users.
If these differences remain unrecognized or unresolved, the resulting design
decisions may not align with stakeholder expectations, undermining trust in AI
systems and potentially causing real-world consequences.
Value alignment in AI-driven systems requires translating abstract human
values into measurable indicators, embedding them into system architecture,
continuously monitoring their adherence, and adapting them dynamically across
contexts and stakeholders.
Self-adaptive socio-technical systems (SASTSs)—especially those driven by
AI—raises serious concerns about the erosion of human autonomy, values, and
ethical control. While such systems optimize decisions automatically, they often
suffer from misaligned objectives, algorithmic bias, and neglect of human values,
particularly “soft ethics” such as personal preferences and cultural norms. The
authors emphasize that current regulatory approaches (e.g., GDPR) are largely
reactive and insufficient, and that systems must instead be designed to actively
empower humans during interaction, rather than treating them as passive users.
