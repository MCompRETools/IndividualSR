### A. Core Definitions

*   **Individual Sustainability:** Refers to the maintenance of the private goods of individual human capital (health, education, skills, knowledge, leadership, and access to services). It encompasses longer-term changes in individuals’ attitudes, aptitudes, and/or behavior, achieved by evaluating consumption against personal and economic well-being. It relies on a capacity for reflection and self-awareness to achieve positive living and psychological well-being, fostering harmony, interconnections, community, ethically responsible systems, and high awareness of values, thoughts, and behaviors.
*   **Human Capital:** Constitutes health, education, skills, knowledge, leadership, and access to services.
*   **Individual Dimension (of Sustainability):** Covers individual freedom, agency (the ability to act in an environment), human dignity, and fulfillment. It includes the ability of individuals to thrive, exercise their rights, develop freely, and possess a value system acknowledging global biological and social systems' interconnectedness.
*   **Individual Sustainability Requirements (ISR):** System-specific requirements derived from underlying human values and human abilities. They serve as an operational bridge to convert abstract values into system-level requirements.
*   **Human Values:** Abstract principles that capture what individuals consider important, playing a central role in shaping decision-making and behavior. They function as guiding criteria for actions, choices, and evaluations. Values can be ethical (e.g., privacy) or non-ethical (e.g., achievement, prestige, wealth, market reputation, public image).
*   **Operationalizing Human Values in Software:** The process of identifying relevant values and translating them into concrete, implementable, and measurable constructs that can be validated and verified within a system.

### B. Key Models and Theories

*   **Maio et al.'s Three-Level Conceptualization of Values:**
    *   **L1 (Value System Level):** Captures patterns and relationships among values held by individuals.
    *   **L2 (Abstract Interpretation Level):** Individuals interpret what a value means in a given context (e.g., "high-quality software" as an expression of achievement).
    *   **L3 (Behavioral Level):** Values are instantiated through concrete actions and system behaviors (e.g., a privacy-prioritizing search engine never logs user queries).

    1.  Realistic and Accurate Perception
    2.  Acceptance of Self, Others, and Nature
    3.  Openness to Experience
    4.  Authenticity and Inner Congruence
    5.  Spontaneity and Naturalness
    6.  Problem-Centered Orientation
    7.  Autonomy and Inner Authority
    8.  Resistance to Social Conditioning
    9.  Desire for Privacy and Reflection
    10. Deep Interpersonal Relationships and Intimacy
    11. Empathy and Identification with Humanity
    12. Democratic and Egalitarian Attitude
    13. Creativity and Expressiveness
    14. Freshness of Appreciation
    15. Peak Experiences
    16. Holistic and Process-Oriented Self
    17. Value System Transformation (towards intrinsic values)
    18. Reduced Materialism
    19. Spiritual or Existential Awareness
    20. Balanced View of Science and Technology
    21. Ecological and Nature-Oriented Awareness

*   **SuSAF Framework for Individual Sustainability (Dimensions & Elicitation Questions):**
    *   **Health:** (Definition: state of mental/physical condition)
        *   Question: How can the product/service improve or worsen a person’s physical, mental, and/or emotional health (e.g., feeling valued/undervalued, respected/disrespected, independent/dependent, or coerced)?
    *   **Lifelong Learning:** (Definition: continuous development via learning opportunities)
        *   Question: How can the product/service affect people’s competencies?
    *   **Privacy:** (Definition: freedom from intrusion in private life)
        *   Question: How can the product/service expose (or help to hide) a person’s identity, whereabouts, or relations?
    *   **Safety:** (Definition: protected from danger, risk, or injury)
        *   Question: How can the product/service expose (or protect) a person from physical harm? How can it make a person feel more (or less) exposed to harm? What if the product/service is used in an unintended way?
    *   **Self-awareness and Free Will:** (Definition: capacity to act or make decisions independently)
        *   Question: How can the product/service empower (or prevent) a person from taking an action or decision when necessary? Can those affected understand its implications, express concerns, or be represented by someone?

### C. Taxonomies / Value Systems

*   **Schwartz Basic Human Values (10 Values):**
    1.  **Self-Direction:** Independent thought and action—choosing, creating, exploring.
    2.  **Stimulation:** Excitement, novelty, challenging in life.
    3.  **Hedonism:** Pleasure or sensuous gratification for oneself.
    4.  **Achievement:** Personal success through demonstrating competence.
    5.  **Power:** Social status and prestige, control or dominance over people and resources.
    6.  **Security:** Safety, harmony and stability of society or self.
    7.  **Conformity:** Restraint of actions, inclinations or impulses.
    8.  **Tradition:** Respect, commitment and acceptance of the customs and ideas.
    9.  **Benevolence:** Preserving and enhancing the welfare of those with whom one is in frequent personal contact.
    10. **Universalism:** Understanding, appreciation, tolerance and protection for the welfare of all people and for nature.

*   **Human Value Themes in Software Context and Mapping to Schwartz Values:**
    *   **Conformity (Rules):** Adherence to rules, standards, norms. (Mapped to: Conformity – Rules)
    *   **Pleasure:** Enjoyment, satisfaction, positive emotional experience. (Mapped to: Hedonism)
    *   **Dignity:** Respectful treatment, avoidance of humiliation. (Mapped to: Face)
    *   **Inclusiveness:** Accommodating diverse user groups, equitable participation. (Mapped to: Universalism – Concern)
    *   **Sense of Belonging:** Feeling socially connected and part of a community. (Mapped to: Security – Personal)
    *   **Freedom:** Ability to make choices, control actions, avoid restrictions. (Mapped to: Self-direction – Action)
    *   **Independence:** Performing tasks without reliance on external systems/support. (Mapped to: Self-direction – Action)
    *   **Wealth:** Financial considerations (cost savings, benefits, ownership). (Mapped to: Power – Resources)
    *   **Privacy:** Protection of user data, personal information, identity. (Mapped to: Self-direction – Action)
    *   **Security:** Safeguarding users from threats, vulnerabilities, risks. (Mapped to: Security – Personal)

*   **System Value Themes in Software Context and Interpreted Mapping to Schwartz Values:**
    *   **Trust:** Perceived reliability, safety, dependability. (Mapped to: Conformity – Interpersonal)
    *   **Correctness:** Accurate, expected, logically consistent outputs. (Mapped to: No clear direct mapping)
    *   **Compatibility:** Effective operation across devices, platforms, environments. (Mapped to: Power – Resources)
    *   **Portability:** Transfer, migration, reuse of data/functionalities. (Mapped to: Self-direction – Action / Power – Resources)
    *   **Reliability:** Stable performance, minimal failures. (Mapped to: No clear direct mapping)
    *   **Efficiency:** Task completion with minimal time, effort, resources. (Mapped to: Power – Resources)
    *   **Energy Preservation:** Optimized energy consumption. (Mapped to: Power – Resources)
    *   **Usability:** Easy to learn, understand, operate. (Mapped to: Benevolence – Caring)
    *   **Accessibility:** Usability for varying abilities, including disabilities. (Mapped to: Benevolence – Caring / Universalism – Concern)
    *   **Longevity:** Functional, maintainable, usable over a long period. (Mapped to: Security – Personal)

### D. Operationalization Logic

*   **Translating Abstract Human Values to Software Requirements (ISR):**
    1.  **Identify Relevant Values:** Pinpoint abstract human values pertinent to the software's context and stakeholders.
    2.  **Translate to Concrete Constructs:** Convert these abstract values into specific, tangible attributes or behaviors that the system should exhibit.
    3.  **Derive System-Level Requirements:** Formulate these concrete constructs into Individual Sustainability Requirements (ISR), which are implementable and measurable specifications.
    4.  **Validate and Verify:** Establish methods to test and ensure that the software system genuinely adheres to the derived ISR and embodies the intended values.

*   **Value Alignment in AI-Driven Systems:**
    1.  **Translate to Measurable Indicators:** Abstract human values (e.g., trust, autonomy) must be defined with quantifiable metrics.
    2.  **Embed in Architecture:** These indicators and the logic to uphold them are integrated directly into the AI system's design and functionality.
    3.  **Continuous Monitoring:** Mechanisms are put in place to track and assess the system's ongoing adherence to specified values.
    4.  **Dynamic Adaptation:** The system is designed to dynamically adjust its behavior and value prioritization, acknowledging that value hierarchies are context-dependent and may shift across stakeholders.

### E. Actionable Knowledge Units

*   **IF** designing software for **Individual Sustainability**.
    *   **THEN** focus on maintaining individual human capital (health, education, skills) and supporting individual freedom, agency, dignity, and fulfillment.
    *   **THEN** design features that foster reflection, self-awareness, harmony, community, ethical systems, and a well-developed, interconnected value system.

*   **IF** eliciting **Individual Sustainability Requirements (ISR)**.
    *   **THEN** identify and consider *all* influential human values, including both strictly ethical (e.g., privacy) and non-ethical (e.g., achievement, market reputation) aspects that guide stakeholder decisions.
    *   **THEN** adopt a domain-specific approach for value elicitation and operationalization due to the context-dependent and dynamic nature of value hierarchies.

*   **IF** operationalizing human values in software.
    *   **THEN** apply Maio et al.'s three-level model:
        1.  Understand the holistic **Value System (L1)**.
        2.  Interpret **Abstract Values (L2)** for the specific software context.
        3.  Translate into **Concrete Actions and System Behaviors (L3)** (e.g., "privacy" means "user queries are never logged").

*   **IF** assessing a product's impact on **Individual Sustainability** using the SuSAF Framework.
    *   **THEN** systematically address its potential effects on: **Health** (physical, mental, emotional well-being, feelings of respect/independence), **Lifelong Learning** (competencies), **Privacy** (identity, whereabouts, relations exposure), **Safety** (physical harm, perceived risk, unintended use), and **Self-awareness & Free Will** (empowerment, understanding implications, expression of concerns).

*   **IF** designing **AI-driven systems**.
    *   **THEN** explicitly acknowledge and manage the **subjective and often conflicting prioritization of values** among diverse stakeholders (developers, users, regulators, civil rights groups).
    *   **THEN** strive to **resolve value differences** early to prevent miscommunication, overlooked needs, undermine trust, or unintended real-world harms.
    *   **THEN** design systems to **actively empower humans** during interaction and control, rather than treating them as passive users, especially for autonomous or self-adaptive socio-technical systems.
    *   **THEN** ensure abstract values are translated into **measurable indicators**, embedded into system architecture, continuously monitored for adherence, and dynamically adapted across contexts and stakeholders.

*   **IF** prioritizing system **efficiency and scalability** in AI design.
    *   **THEN** be aware that this may conflict with user values such as **transparency in decision-making, equity, or preservation of autonomy**.

*   **IF** designing highly **secure systems**.
    *   **THEN** be prepared for potential **trade-offs with ease of use** for end-users.

### F. Model Memory Summary

**Individual Sustainability** entails fostering human capital, well-being, freedom, and ethical behavior through long-term changes. **ISR** operationalize **Human Values** (abstract, context-dependent principles, ethical/non-ethical) into measurable software requirements. The **Schwartz Basic Human Values** offer a taxonomy that maps to software-relevant human (e.g., Privacy, Dignity) and system (e.g., Efficiency, Usability) themes. For **sustainable software design, especially AI systems**, critical actions involve: translating values to measurable, monitored, and adaptable system architectures; recognizing and resolving conflicting stakeholder value priorities; and designing systems that actively empower human agency over passive use. This requires balancing technical priorities (e.g., efficiency, security) with human-centric values (e.g., autonomy, transparency, usability).
